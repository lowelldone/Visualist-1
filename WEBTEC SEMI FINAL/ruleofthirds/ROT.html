THE RULE OF THIRDS


What is Rule Of Thirds?

The rule of thirds is a fundamental composition technique in photography and design. It suggests positioning your subject in the left or right third of the frame while leaving the remaining two-thirds more open. This approach often results in visually appealing and balanced compositions. To apply the rule of thirds, imagine dividing your photo or camera's viewfinder into nine equal sections with two horizontal and two vertical lines, creating a grid. Most cameras and smartphones offer this grid as a selectable feature. As Ingersoll describes, "Think of it like The Brady Bunch intro with nine identical rectangles arranged in a three-by-three grid—three rows and three columns." The intersection points where the lines cross are ideal spots to position your subject or focal point. Using these intersections helps balance the subject with surrounding negative space, guiding the viewer’s eye and creating a compelling visual composition.

Let's Learn What is Grid

Grids are lines that divide your frame into equal or proportional sections, such as thirds, quarters, or squares. They act as a guide to help you align subjects and elements within your frame, fostering balance, harmony, and visual interest. Most cameras and smartphones offer a grid option that can be toggled on or off in the settings, making it easier to compose visually appealing shots. A common application of grids is the rule of thirds, where the frame is divided into nine equal sections using two vertical and two horizontal imaginary lines. This creates three columns, three rows, and nine segments. In this method, important compositional elements and leading lines are strategically placed along these lines or at their intersections, drawing the viewer's attention and enhancing the image's visual impact. The rule of thirds is particularly useful for creating dynamic, balanced, and engaging compositions.

Landscape Orientation

When taking a landscape photo, it’s common to center the horizon. But for a more interesting composition, try placing the horizon on the top or bottom third of the dividing line. If the main focus is on land, like mountains or buildings, place the horizon near the top third. If the sky is the focus, like in a sunset, place the horizon near the bottom third. This can make the photo feel more balanced and visually appealing.

Portrait Orientation

A portrait using the rule of thirds involves dividing the image into a 3x3 grid and placing the subject, often the eyes, along the lines or at their intersections. This technique creates a balanced and dynamic composition by avoiding a centered subject. It guides the viewer’s eye naturally across the frame, using negative space to add depth and interest. The rule of thirds enhances the overall aesthetic, making the portrait more engaging and visually appealing.

The subject’s face is positioned along the vertical and horizontal lines, with eyes as the focal point, creating a dramatic and balanced composition.

TIP

Think of the rule of thirds grid as you encounter various scenes throughout your day. For instance, you can think about how to apply the grid in your mind’s eye when you’re stopped at a red light on your way home from work. Or even at home, you can visualize the rule by thinking about the grid covering scenes like your child playing, a fruit bowl placed on your counter, or what you can see from your front porch. Remember, sometimes the subject or point of view you’re looking for may not be right in front of you. You may have to move around to find that perfect composition!
